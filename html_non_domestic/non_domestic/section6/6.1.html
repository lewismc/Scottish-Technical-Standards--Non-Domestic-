6.1.0 Introduction
Standard 6.1 focuses on the reduction of carbon dioxide emissions arising
from the use of heating, hot water, ventilation and lighting in a new building. It
sets an overall level for maximum carbon dioxide emissions in buildings by
use of a methodology which incorporates a range of parameters that
influence energy use. This means a designer is obliged to consider energy
performance as a complete package rather than looking only at individual
elements such as insulation or boiler efficiency - a ‘whole building approach’
to energy, which offers a significant degree of design flexibility.
For the majority of new buildings , standard 6.1 has the greatest influence on
design for energy performance. Standards 6.2 to 6.10, in the main,
recommend benchmark and backstop levels to be achieved for individual
elements or systems. To achieve compliance with standard 6.1, it will be
necessary to improve on these backstop levels or incorporate additional
energy efficiency measures, such as low carbon equipment (LCE).
small stand-alone
buildings
The guidance given in support of standards 6.2 to 6.8 for extensions and

than 50 m 2 .

6.1.1 Simplified Building Energy Model (SBEM)
Simplified Building
Energy Model
The Simplified Building Energy Model (SBEM) is a calculation tool which may
be used with the methodology which conforms with European Directive
2002/91/EC and is approved for use with the carbon dioxide emissions
calculation. SBEM has a basic user interface, iSBEM, which
includes Scottish compliance parameters for use with this guidance and is
available on the National Calculation Methodology website.
Alternative calculation
tools
Other tools may be used with the methodology (such as dynamic simulation
modelling), particularly where the building is considered to be a complex
design. In this situation, the verifier should be consulted early in the design
process. A list of other approved calculation tools can be found on the
'section 6 software' page of the Building Standards Division website. The
guidance given here is written in terms of the SBEM calculation tool but the
principles and procedures also apply to other calculation tools.
6.1.2 Summary of procedure
Objectives
The calculated carbon dioxide emissions measured in kilograms per square
metre of floor area per annum for the proposed building should not exceed
those which are calculated for a ‘notional' building which has the same size
and shape. In broad terms, the way that this process works is described
below:
TER
Input the size, shape, construction and fixed building services data of the
proposed building into a calculation tool which has, embedded within it, the
Scottish standard package of construction and building services performance
measures.
A carbon dioxide emission rate for the ‘notional’ building is generated based
upon a building designed to meet 2002 standards. This is then improved
upon by using a formula, given in clause 6.1.7, which reflects
the ventilation/cooling stategy for the building . This creates the target (carbon
dioxide) emission rate (TER).
BER
The input data of the proposed building should have regard to:
•
the actual type(s) of fuel to be used in the building and their CO 2
emission factors; and
•
the benchmark and backstop levels identified in guidance to standards
6.2, 6.3, 6.4, 6.5 and 6.6.
Once the designer is satisfied that all the input data accurately reflects the
proposed building design, the building (carbon dioxide) emission rate (BER)
can be calculated.

6.1.7 Creating the target carbon dioxide emission rate (TER)
Once a carbon dioxide emissions rate is generated for the ‘notional’ building
'C notional ', the TER can be calculated by applying one of the improvement
formulae below in the calculation tool, depending on the building services
strategy of the proposed building .
For heated and naturally ventilated buildings (or parts thereof) use:
TER = C notional x (1 - 0.15) x (1- 0.15) x (1- 0.26)
This represents a 30% improvement on 2007 and a 47% improvement on
2002.
For heated and mechanically ventilated (including air-conditioned) buildings
(or parts thereof) use:
TER = C notional x (1 – 0.20) x (1- 0.15) x (1- 0.26)
This represents a 30% improvement on 2007 and a 50% improvement on
2002.
The product of these three separate multipliers gives an overall carbon
dioxide emission reduction improvement factor, which is applied to C notional to
give the TER. The three multipliers represent: the improvements on 2002
standards made by the 2007 guidance; a localised or building integrated low
carbon equipment (LCE) benchmark; and the 2010 improvement factor
needed to deliver a 30% improvement on previous standards. The latter is
lower than 30% due to the increase in the LCE benchmark improvement
factor.
LCE includes the following types of technologies:
•
photovoltaics;
•
solar water heating;
•
wind turbines;
•
CHP;
•
district or block heating; and
•
heat pumps.
The LCE benchmark has the effect of applying a discrete improvement factor
in recognition of both Directive 2002/91/EC on the energy performance of
buildings and Directive 2009/28/EC which promotes the use of energy from
renewable sources. This is intended to encourage designers to consider the
incorporation of low carbon equipment (LCE) within proposals.
Designers may choose to include more LCE than this benchmark, although
the extent to which this can be traded off against measures such as thermal

6.1.8 Calculating the building carbon dioxide emission rate (BER)
The BER is calculated by replacing the embedded values and efficiencies in
SBEM calculation tool with those that the designer wishes to incorporate in
the design. There are however measures that restrain the flexibility of design
and these are:
•
the type(s) of fuel to be used in the proposed building and allied CO 2
emission factors (see note below);
•
backstop measures given in the guidance to standards 6.2 to 6.6, where
appropriate; and
•
any display window U-value should be taken as 5.7 W/m 2 K.
The first of these measures is to encourage designers to select low carbon
dioxide emitting fuels where possible and the second is to help reduce
energy demand (particularly where LCE may reduce CO 2 emissions but not
energy consumption).
Information on carbon dioxide equivalent emission factors is given in the
'NCM Modelling Guide For Scotland'

6.1.10 Shell and fit out buildings
Where, rather than making a staged warrant application, a new building shell
and fit out are the subject of separate building warrant applications, the final
specification of building systems may not be known. However it remains
important to ensure that such a building , if intended to be heated or cooled
(other than by heating provided solely for the purpose of frost protection) will
still be constructed to limit carbon dioxide emissions.
In such cases, the calculation methodology should still be used to show that
the building shell, as proposed, will comply with standard 6.1. This can be
done by adopting one of the default ‘worst case’ services specifications within
the calculation tool for any unknowns not forming part of the shell works ,
such as heating systems, mechanical ventilation and lighting, and then
making the TER/BER comparison. In calculating the BER, reference should
be made to the maximum U-values , thermal bridging values and air-tightness
levels identified in clauses 6.2.4 to 6.2.6.
Continuing requirement
Where standard 6.1 applies to a building , a continuing requirement can be
issued with a building warrant for a shell building . This can ensure that the
subsequent fit out, whether subject to a building warrant or not, has to
demonstrate that the final building also complies with standard 6.1. This will
also result in the production, under standard 6.9, of an Energy Performance
Certificate for the fitted out building , on completion.
Dependant upon the final specification of the building, additional provisions
may be needed to meet the carbon dioxide emissions standard at building fit
out phase. Accordingly, those involved in the further development of a shell
building are advised to consider early assessment of the building , as
constructed , to determine the extent to which such provisions may be needed
at fit out.
Similarly, when considering either separate building warrants for shell and fit
out or a single, staged warrant, an early assessment of the implications each
route may have on the design and specification of the initial building is
recommended.
